What to Expect During a Home Inspection in Prince George

By Mike Webber · Ironclad Inspections · Prince George, BC · June 2026

You've found a home you love in Prince George, your offer has been accepted, and your realtor mentions you should get a home inspection done before subjects are removed. But if you've never been through one before, you might be wondering — what actually happens? How long does it take? Should you be there? And what do you do with the report when you get it?

This guide walks you through the entire process, start to finish, so you know exactly what to expect on inspection day.

"A home inspection isn't a pass or fail. It's about getting the full picture of what you're buying — so there are no expensive surprises after moving day."

Before the Inspection: What to Do to Prepare

Once you've booked your inspection, there are a few simple things you can do to make the day go smoothly.

Before Inspection Day

  • Confirm access to the property with your realtor
  • Make sure utilities are on — gas, hydro, and water need to be active
  • Ask the seller to clear access to the attic hatch, electrical panel, and crawlspace if possible
  • Write down any specific concerns you already have about the property
  • Plan to attend at least the final walkthrough — more on this below

How Long Does a Home Inspection Take?

For a typical single-family home in Prince George, expect the inspection to take between 3.5 and 4.5 hours. Larger homes, older properties, or homes with crawlspaces and complex systems may take longer.

This isn't a quick once-over. A thorough inspection means working methodically through every system of the home — and in a city like Prince George where older neighbourhoods like Spruceland, Nechako, and the Bowl have homes dating back several decades, that time is well spent.

What Does the Inspector Actually Check?

A complete home inspection covers every major system and component of the property. Here's what gets examined:

🏗️

Structure & Foundation

The foundation, framing, and structural components are assessed for signs of movement, cracking, water intrusion, or settlement — common concerns in Northern BC given our freeze-thaw cycles.

🏠

Exterior & Roof

Siding, windows, doors, drainage, grading, and the roof are all inspected. Prince George winters are hard on roofs and exterior cladding — this section often reveals deferred maintenance.

⚡

Electrical System

The panel, wiring, outlets, and fixtures are checked for safety and code concerns. Older homes in Prince George sometimes have outdated panels like Federal Pacific or aluminum wiring that warrant attention.

🚿

Plumbing

Visible plumbing, water pressure, drainage, and the hot water tank are all assessed. Polybutylene pipe — common in homes built between the late 1970s and mid-1990s — is flagged when present.

🔥

Heating & Cooling (HVAC)

The furnace, ductwork, and any cooling systems are inspected and functionally tested. In Prince George's climate, a reliable heating system isn't optional — this gets careful attention.

🔬

Thermal Imaging — Included at No Extra Cost

Infrared scanning is used to detect hidden moisture, insulation deficiencies, and electrical hotspots that can't be seen with the naked eye. Many inspectors charge extra for this — at Ironclad it's always included.

🏚️

Attic & Crawlspace

These are often where the most significant issues hide — inadequate insulation, moisture, pest activity, or ventilation problems. Both are accessed and thoroughly inspected.

Should You Attend the Inspection?

You don't have to be there for the entire inspection, but you absolutely should attend the final 30-minute walkthrough at the end.

This is where your inspector walks you through the property in person, shows you what was found, explains what's significant and what isn't, and answers your questions face to face. It's a completely different experience from reading a report — you can see things with your own eyes, ask follow-up questions on the spot, and leave with a genuine understanding of the home you're about to buy.

For first-time buyers in Prince George especially, the walkthrough is worth its weight in gold. You'll also learn where your shutoffs are, how your furnace works, and what maintenance to plan for — practical knowledge that pays off for years.

"Most buyers who attend the walkthrough tell me it was the most useful hour of the entire home-buying process."

What Happens After the Inspection?

Within the same day — usually by that evening — you'll receive your full digital inspection report via email. Ironclad uses Spectora, which produces a clean, mobile-friendly report with photos attached to every finding.

The report is organized by system and clearly flags items by severity. You'll be able to share it instantly with your realtor, mortgage broker, or lawyer.

Using the Report to Negotiate

If the inspection reveals significant issues — a furnace near end of life, evidence of water intrusion, or an aging roof — your realtor can use these findings to negotiate with the seller before subjects are removed. This might mean a price reduction, a repair credit, or asking the seller to fix specific items before closing.

This is one of the most practical reasons to get an inspection done. Even in a competitive Prince George market, significant deficiencies give buyers legitimate grounds to renegotiate.

What If the Report Comes Back Clean?

Every report has something in it — no house is perfect. The goal isn't a clean report; it's an honest one. Minor maintenance items are normal and expected. What you're really watching for are safety concerns, major structural or system issues, or signs of water damage — the things that cost real money or pose real risk.

A clean report is still valuable. It gives you confidence in your purchase and a clear maintenance roadmap going forward.

A Few Things Specific to Prince George Homes

Having inspected homes throughout Northern BC, there are a handful of issues that come up more frequently in Prince George than in other markets — things worth knowing about before you buy.

Freeze-thaw foundation movement. Our climate is hard on foundations. Look for stepped cracking in masonry, sticking doors, or uneven floors — all potential signs of foundation movement worth investigating.

Older electrical panels. Many homes in established Prince George neighbourhoods still have panels that insurers are reluctant to cover. Federal Pacific Stab-Lok panels in particular are flagged as fire risks and are commonly found in homes built between the 1950s and 1980s.

Moisture in crawlspaces. With our significant snowfall and spring melt, crawlspace moisture is a recurring finding. Left unchecked it leads to mould, wood rot, and pest activity.

Heating system age. A furnace that's 20+ years old in Prince George's climate is a significant consideration. Replacement costs and the timing of failure in a northern winter are worth factoring into your offer.

Roof condition. Heavy snow loads and ice damming take a toll on Prince George roofs. The age and condition of the roof is one of the most important things an inspection will tell you.
